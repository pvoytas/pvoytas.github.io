Technical Talk Guidelines for Jr/Sr Seminar

 

There are actually many kinds of “technical” talks.  What follows is a brief non-exhaustive summary of the kinds of technical talks you might run into, followed by some good practices.

 

Public talk: Audience that generally has little to no technical background. You can’t assume much background knowledge or facility with physics/math concepts. These are hard to do well and honestly. (45-60 min)

 

Colloquium: Larger departments often have weekly speakers from other institutions present on cutting edge physics they are working on, but to non-specialists. So the audience is physics faculty, scientists, post-docs, grad-students, undergrad students from all fields of physics. (45-60 min)

 

Seminar: This would be like a colloquium, but the audience would be specialists in your field (e.g. I might be presenting to only the nuclear physics research group when visiting a university). The content would be more specialized and I would assume familiarity with many nuclear physics ideas that physicists in other specialty areas wouldn’t be familiar with. (45-60 min)

 

Conference Talk (non-invited, non-plenary): Usually a highly technical and narrowly focused presentation of your results on a project. Audience is usually specialists, often working on something similar. Almost no time is available for motivation. (10-12 min)

 

Journal Club talk: A group of people at your institution who present summaries of research articles to each other. This is to keep everyone abreast of recent interesting or relevant work in a particular field. Often divided up by journal (you peruse Phys Rev C, I’ll do Journal of Physics G, someone else covers NIM …). Often short, often several per meeting.

 

Group presentation: These are usually highly technical and narrowly focused updates to your research group on a project you are working on.

 

 

 

 

For the purposes of Jr/Sr Seminar, we will be looking at talks that are closest to the Conference/ Seminar/Journal Club types

 

Good practices:

Mechanics:

Generally you’ll be using powerpoint or something similar.

 

Length: A good rule of thumb is that it takes about one minute (average) per reasonably composed slide.

 

Don’t use distracting backgrounds or effects

 

Make sure the contrast is such that everything is easily readable

 

Make sure that the size of everything you present is easily readable from the back of whatever room you will be presenting in. This is especially important for axes values, labels, and notations.

 

Use equation editor

 

Use good drawing tools (Word drawing tools at minimum, inkscape or equivalent better)

 

Learn how to copy figures you refer to at good resolution and at good size.

 

Don’t write out full sentences

 

If you use them, use bulleted lists hierarchically

 

Keep slides neat and clean and uncluttered

 

Generally don’t show numbers (i.e. don’t substitute numerical values for variables) in equations (unless there’s some specific point like comparing the size of terms—even then there are probably better ways).

 

Never rely only on what you say orally to convey important results or points. Humans are distractible. If someone in the audience zones out when you say something crucial, they will be lost for the rest of the talk. Put it on the slides as well so they stand a chance to stay with the rest of the talk.

 

Speak loudly, clearly, and slowly. No, even more loudly, clearly, and slowly than that.

 

Don’t trail off or speed up at the end of sentences.

 

Content:

Know your audience and target the level appropriately. For seminar, this means target an audience like you: Jr/Sr undergrad physics major.

 

Find a storyline or thread to organize your talk. There are many different ways you could present the same material. One of the most important things is to organize things to make it easy for your audience to follow. Talks are best if they have a natural feeling of flow: one thing leading fairly smoothly (in the logical-progression sense) from another. Remember, the point of you presenting something is to make easy for the audience what you worked hard at understanding.

 

Avoid jumping around. If you need to refer to an earlier slide, insert a copy of it near the slide you are on—memory is cheap.

 

Know what you present on and don’t present on what you don’t know.

 

Minimize “hand waving” (see previous point). Faculty probably set bad examples of this for one of several reasons. 1) Over your head/distracting from main point 2) I don’t know for sure, but I can imagine how it might work b/c I know a lot of physics 3) Arrogance

 

Think back to what you didn’t know when you were researching your topic—your audience will likely no know that too and it’s your job to make it clear.

 

Don’t deliberately raise questions you can’t answer.

 

It is usually better to say “I don’t know” or “I haven’t thought about that so I’m not sure” in answer to a question than to pretend you know when you don’t. At least be sure to hedge because thinking through something you haven’t thought about before while standing in front of a large group of people is the second most dangerous thing in the world you can do.
