Low Temperature Physics

Robbie Kehl 11/05/08

Low-temperature physics is the science concerned with the production and maintenance of temperatures much below normal, down to almost absolute zero, and with various phenomena that occur only at such temperatures especially at temperatures so low that the quantum character of the substance becomes observable in effects such as superconductivity, superfluid liquid helium, magnetic cooling, and nuclear orientation.  Low-temperature physics is also known as cryogenics.  This field was furthered extensively during World War II when scientists found that metals cooled to very low temperatures showed more resistance to wear, thus cryogenic hardening was started. Using this similar process we have done this to many materials and created better acoustics, tools, engines, firearms and much more. We can even create cryogenic fuels such as liquid hydrogen.  We use successive magnetization and demagnetization under the proper combination of conditions which can  lower the temperature to only about a millionth of a degree above absolute zero.  Getting lower temperatures means finding a way to remove more and more energy, which becomes very difficult.  When metals get less then a nanokelvin above absolute zero, then superconductivity occurs.  You can generate a current in a superconductive wire loop for years and years and it will not have any significant decay.  They lose their electrical resistivity completely. Liquids at this temperature become superfluids.  They have no resistance to flow under certain conditions.  There are many complicated theories and ideas formed about low-temperature physics.  Some are the Josephson Effect, Bose-Einstein condensation, the quantum Hall effect, and Quantum coherence in mesoscopic  and nanoscopic systems. Low-temperature physics also deals with thermometry and heat transfer of systems.  Some applications of this are superconductivity for large magnets and low noise and high sensitivity instrumentation.  These instruments have helped research in the field of biophysics, and in the search for magnetic monopoles and quarks.  MRI’s and particle accelerators are a few major applications resulting from low-temperature physics.  The applications of this are slow in making, because of the difficulty in acquiring the materials needed to create mass amounts.  Continual research in this field could lead to superconducting motors and generators as well as superconducting power lines.  The future of low-temperature physics is headed toward dilute gas Bose condensates, unconventional materials, and many ideas in the field of nanoscience.
