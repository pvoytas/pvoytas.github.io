Astronomy

Erin Kapp (10/29/08)

 

Astronomy is usually defined as "the study of all celestial objects."

 

Often the terms astronomy and astrophysics are conflated, but if a distinction is drawn, it's generally that 'astronomy' deals with observational measurements and data, and 'astrophysics' deals with more theoretical pursuits (cosmology, stellar evolution, relativity); astronomy leans toward the phenomenological side of things, while astrophysics leans toward the quantitative side.

 

Since the things observed in astronomy are so widely varied (other planets, black holes, stars, galaxies...), astronomy is often broken down into subcategories based on what is observed.

 

Radio/microwave astronomy --- pulsars, black holes, dead stars (radio telescopes)

Ultraviolet/optical/infrared --- gas clouds/dust, galaxies/individual stars (telescope + CCD camera)

Xray/gamma ray --- matter falling into a black hole, massive star explosions (satellite + CCD/counter)

=>sort of astrophysics: non-EM --- subatomic particles, gravity waves (specialized observatories)

 

Some current research questions:

 

- NRAO measured a young galaxy and found its magnetic field was much stronger than Earth's, when they expected the opposite- challenges the dynamo theory of cosmic magnetic field generation. - also first instance of measuring the strength of a galaxy’s magnetic field using the Zeeman effect.

 

- Radio astronomers found a pulsar going "in the wrong kind of orbit around what appears to be the wrong kind of star" - pulsar could have been stripped from a nearby cluster, could be just unusual, could be the first pulsar in a triple star system - part of a survey project using the Arecibo radio telescope

 

- Exoplanets: how many are there, where are they, what kind of planet are they, could there be life on them?
