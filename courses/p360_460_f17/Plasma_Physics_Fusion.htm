Plasma Physics and Fusion

Fusion is simply the joining together of nuclei to form a heavier nucleus. This is what occurs in the sun, where hydrogen is fused to form helium. Due to the binding energies, fusion is able to produce far more energy than fission. However, there are electrostatic forces between positively charged nuclei that have to be overcome in order for the nuclear forces to take effect. This means it requires a lot of energy to get this energy. Modern fusion reactors work at 100s of millions of degrees Celsius. The most advanced type is called the tokamak. These use a more efficient fusion reaction involving deuterium (H2) and tritium (H3) which fuse to make helium (He4) and a neutron. At such high temperatures, the deuterium and tritium are ions in a plasma state. These charged particles are able to interact with a magnetic field. The plasma is kept in a torus or donut shape in a vacuum by using magnetic fields. The magnetic fields also create a current in the plasma, causing it to heat to high enough temperatures for fusion to take place. As far as getting energy out, we aren’t there yet. The amount of energy given off by fusion isn’t even enough to even sustain the fusion. The Joint European Torus (JET), for example, is only able to produce an output 65% of its inputted energy.

There is vast potential in fusion as a power source. Theoretically, it provides a great amount of energy. Its fuels are abundant, deuterium can be extracted from all forms of water, and tritium can be “bred” from lithium. There is enough lithium on the earth to last at least a thousand years using exclusively fusion to feed the world’s power needs. In fact, the lifetime supply of energy for a person from an industrialized country can be found in 500 L of water and 30 grams of Lithium. It is very safe: specific conditions are needed for fusion to take place. A reactor won’t become uncontrollable, but the plasma will cool. Also, there are no greenhouse gas emissions, nor radioactive waste produced.

The sun is proof of this potential, which we have seen is not nearly tapped yet (we still can’t get out what we put in). The fusion power field is nearly dominated by ITER, originally standing for International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor, now simply meaning Latin for “journey” or “direction.” There are job opportunities for both US and international research (U.S. ITER and ITER). The largest current ITER experiment is the JET, near Culham, Oxfordshire in the UK, with a larger one to begin construction in France in 2009. There are several other tokamaks for research in the US including the NSTX at Princeton, Alcator C-Mod at MIT, and DIII-D in San Diego. Job opportunities for both US and international research can be found at usiter.org
