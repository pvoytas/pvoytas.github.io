1. a) Show that when the recoil of the daughter nucleus in gamma decay is considered, a better relationship between the energy of the gamma and the transition energy is Eγ ≈ ΔE – (ΔE)2/(2mc2), where m is the mass of the daughter and ΔE is the transition energy. Start with conservation of momentum (pdc=pγc) and conservation of energy (ΔE=Eγ+Kd), where the d subscript indicates the daughter. You’ll have to use the fact that mc2 >> pdc as well as the fact that to first order Eγ = ΔE in order to make some approximations that will lead to the answer above. Hint: think Taylor series expansion.

b) Following the decay of 198Au to 198Hg, three different γ’s can be detected. Their energies in keV (with experimental uncertainties) are

Eγ1:  411.80441 ± 0.00015

Eγ2:  675.88743 ± 0.00069

Eγ3: 1087.69033 ± 0.00074

One interpretation is that there are two excited states of 198Hg that are populated in the decay, and that the γ’s correspond respectively to the transitions S1→S0, S2→S1, and S2→S0 (where S0 is the ground state and S1 and S2 are the excited states). If this is true, then we expect ΔE1 + ΔE2 = ΔE3.  Using the simple approximation Eγ = ΔE, this is not true, even taking the experimental uncertainties into account. Show that including the nuclear recoil from the gamma decays resolves the discrepancy (you can use what you found in part a) to do the calculation).

 

2. a) A nucleus has the following levels, starting with the ground states and going up in energy:

3/2+, 7/2+, 5/2+, ½-, and 3/2-. Draw a level scheme showing the possible γ transitions between each level (consider all pairs of levels) and label all possible multipoles for each transition.

b) Are there any of these transitions that can’t be made by γ decay? If so, how might these excited states decay?

 

3. An excited state decays to a known 1/2+ ground state; the multipolarity of the transition is found to be E2. What are the possible spin and parity assignments for the excited state?

 

4. In a rotational band, the states lowest in energy are 0+, 2+, and 4+ (from lowest to highest). We find experimentally that the 4+ state decays to the 2+ state rather than directly to the ground state.

a) What do you expect the dominant multipolarity (E1, M1, E2,…) of the decay from 4+ to 2+ to be?

b) What do you expect the dominant multipolarity of the decay from 2+ to 0+ to be?

c) Justify the observation that the 4+ state primarily decays to the 2+ state by estimating (using the Weisskopf single-particle estimates) the ratio between the probability of the decay in a) to the probability of the decay in b). (Note: since these are rotational states, the Weisskopf estimates should not be taken too seriously.)
