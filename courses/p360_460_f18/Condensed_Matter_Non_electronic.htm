Condensed Matter Physics:                                                                                                        James Duff

 

Condensed Matter physics is the broadest range of modern physics.  It deals with macro- and micro-scopic physical properties of matter.  This can deal with state theory, such as with conventional solids and liquids, or more exotic Bose-Einstein Condensates and Superfluids.  There are also branches that deal with electromagnetic properties of matter, like semiconductors, insulators, and going into phenomena like superconductivity.  One odd form of matter is the liquid crystal, which are generally large polarizable organic molecules that will flow like a liquid, but like to line up with a specific orientation.  The fact that they are usually large organic molecules oriented in one general direction means they can polarize light, filtering light until it only orientations that match the orientation of the molecules gets through.  The crystals can be aligned to allow more or less light through by applying an electric field, which is generally how LCD monitors work.  Because Lipids (fats) act a lot like liquid crystals, research in this area is fairly useful for biologists in that area.  Another area of interest is in granular solids, like sand.  These materials behave like a liquid under normal circumstances.  The motion of a single grain is fairly easy to understand, but the motion of many grains can become hard to predict because of the fluid motion and the individual motion of the grains due to things like particle size.  The granular nature of the material results in some interesting problems in statistical mechanics, and some research is being done on the dynamics of dunes and avalanches.  Both liquid crystals and granular materials behave in ways that are hard to describe, they also make for good nonlinear dynamics media, and are areas of research in that area as well.
